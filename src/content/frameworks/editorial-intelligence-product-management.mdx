Product teams do not have an information problem.

They have a synthesis problem.

Customer interviews, product analytics, support conversations, sales feedback, feature requests, community discussions and market signals accumulate continuously — across different tools, different teams and different formats.

Most of it never gets compared. Rarely does it compound.

Editorial Intelligence helps product teams capture, connect, synthesise and activate this evidence. The result is not simply better content. It is better product decisions, stronger prioritisation, clearer product narratives and knowledge that improves over time.

The problem is not that product teams lack evidence. It is that the evidence arrives fragmented, unevenly weighted and difficult to interpret collectively.

A single loud customer request sits alongside dozens of quiet signals pointing in a different direction. A recurring support pattern contradicts a roadmap assumption. Analytics show low adoption of a feature that sales is promising to prospects.

Without a system to connect and interpret these signals, product decisions rely on the evidence that is most recent, most vocal or most convenient — not the evidence that is most meaningful.

## Five product problems Editorial Intelligence helps solve

<div class="problem-cards">
  <div class="problem-card">
    <span class="problem-num">01</span>
    <h3 class="problem-title">Fragmented customer evidence</h3>
    <p class="problem-body">Research, sales feedback, analytics and support data often live in separate systems. Editorial Intelligence connects them.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="problem-card">
    <span class="problem-num">02</span>
    <h3 class="problem-title">Weak prioritisation context</h3>
    <p class="problem-body">Roadmaps can become lists of requests rather than expressions of customer and strategic need. Editorial Intelligence makes the reasoning behind priorities visible.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="problem-card">
    <span class="problem-num">03</span>
    <h3 class="problem-title">Lost organisational memory</h3>
    <p class="problem-body">Teams repeatedly rediscover insights because previous research, decisions and outcomes are difficult to retrieve. Editorial Intelligence creates knowledge that compounds.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="problem-card">
    <span class="problem-num">04</span>
    <h3 class="problem-title">Product and marketing disconnect</h3>
    <p class="problem-body">Product teams understand what was built. Marketing teams need to understand why it matters. Editorial Intelligence creates a shared evidence base and narrative.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="problem-card">
    <span class="problem-num">05</span>
    <h3 class="problem-title">AI-generated signal overload</h3>
    <p class="problem-body">AI can summarise thousands of conversations, but volume does not equal understanding. Editorial Intelligence adds human interpretation, challenge and judgement.</p>
  </div>
</div>

## The Editorial Intelligence product workflow

<div class="workflow-steps">
  <div class="workflow-step">
    <span class="workflow-num">1</span>
    <div class="workflow-content">
      <h3 class="workflow-title">Capture</h3>
      <p class="workflow-body">Collect product evidence from research, analytics, support, sales and market sources.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="workflow-step">
    <span class="workflow-num">2</span>
    <div class="workflow-content">
      <h3 class="workflow-title">Structure</h3>
      <p class="workflow-body">Tag evidence by customer, problem, product area, market and strategic theme.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="workflow-step">
    <span class="workflow-num">3</span>
    <div class="workflow-content">
      <h3 class="workflow-title">Connect</h3>
      <p class="workflow-body">Identify relationships across sources rather than treating each signal in isolation.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="workflow-step">
    <span class="workflow-num">4</span>
    <div class="workflow-content">
      <h3 class="workflow-title">Synthesise</h3>
      <p class="workflow-body">Turn recurring evidence into themes, tensions, hypotheses and emerging patterns.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="workflow-step">
    <span class="workflow-num">5</span>
    <div class="workflow-content">
      <h3 class="workflow-title">Decide</h3>
      <p class="workflow-body">Use the evidence to support prioritisation, roadmap and product strategy discussions.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="workflow-step">
    <span class="workflow-num">6</span>
    <div class="workflow-content">
      <h3 class="workflow-title">Activate</h3>
      <p class="workflow-body">Turn the decision context into briefs, narratives, launch materials and organisational knowledge.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

<p class="workflow-note">AI can accelerate the first four stages — capture, structure, connect and synthesise. Human judgement remains essential when interpreting ambiguity, weighing evidence and making trade-offs.</p>

## Example: from feature requests to product insight

<div class="example-block">
  <div class="example-signals">
    <p class="example-label">Raw signals</p>
    <ul class="example-list">
      <li>Customers repeatedly request more automation</li>
      <li>Support tickets show confusion around setup</li>
      <li>Usage data shows low adoption of an existing automation feature</li>
      <li>Sales teams report that competitors appear easier to use</li>
    </ul>
  </div>

  <div class="example-conclusions">
    <div class="example-weak">
      <p class="example-conclusion-label">Weak conclusion</p>
      <p class="example-conclusion-text">"Customers want more automation."</p>
    </div>
    <div class="example-strong">
      <p class="example-conclusion-label">Editorial Intelligence conclusion</p>
      <p class="example-conclusion-text">"The primary issue may not be a lack of automation. Customers may not understand, discover or trust the automation already available."</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="example-implications">
    <p class="example-label">Possible product implications</p>
    <ul class="example-list">
      <li>Improve onboarding</li>
      <li>Change product language</li>
      <li>Simplify configuration</li>
      <li>Test trust and explainability</li>
      <li>Reassess whether a new feature is actually needed</li>
    </ul>
  </div>
</div>

This example shows the difference between summarising evidence and interpreting it. The raw signals were all present. What changed was the editorial judgement applied to what they collectively meant.

## The future product manager is partly an editor

As AI reduces the effort required to collect, transcribe and summarise product evidence, the product manager's role shifts.

The differentiating work becomes:

- Selecting which evidence deserves attention
- Challenging weak interpretations
- Identifying contradictions
- Separating recurring patterns from loud individual requests
- Creating a coherent explanation of what the team should do next

This is editorial judgement applied to product decisions.

The product manager who can interpret evidence — not just collect it — will become increasingly valuable as the cost of collection falls.
