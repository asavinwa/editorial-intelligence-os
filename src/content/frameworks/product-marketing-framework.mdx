*This framework describes how Editorial Intelligence applies to product marketing specifically — how research becomes narrative, and how narrative becomes the shared foundation for launches, customer evidence, sales enablement and multi-channel campaigns.*

---

## The problem with most product marketing

Product marketing is often described as a coordination problem.

The research team produces findings. The product team writes copy. The content team produces editorial. Sales creates its own deck. Customer marketing runs its own evidence programme. Each function works from a different brief, on a different timeline, toward a different audience.

The channels are coordinated — scheduled into a launch plan, assigned owners, tracked in a content calendar. But the underlying material is not shared. Each function starts from its own version of the story.

The result is fragmentation that looks like activity. A lot is produced. Very little compounds.

The fragmentation is structural, not operational. You cannot fix it with better project management or more frequent cross-functional meetings. The problem is not coordination. It is the absence of a shared narrative foundation that every function draws from.

---

## What a narrative foundation changes

A narrative foundation is not a messaging house or a set of approved brand statements.

It is a strategic argument — a specific, evidenced claim about the market, the customer's situation, and what that means for how the product should be positioned.

When product marketing is built from a narrative foundation:

- The launch press release, the thought leadership article, the sales positioning and the customer case study all tell versions of the same story — because they share the same underlying evidence and argument
- New content is faster to produce because the context is already established
- Evidence accumulates rather than being commissioned fresh each campaign
- The organisation develops a recognisable point of view rather than producing activity that disappears between campaigns

The question is how to build and maintain that foundation. This is what Editorial Intelligence is designed to do.

## The Editorial Intelligence model for product marketing

The EI cycle produces four outputs that directly serve product marketing:

### 1. The launch narrative

A product launch is not primarily a product announcement. It is a claim about the market.

The most durable product launches connect the product to a problem the audience already recognises — one that the launch narrative names precisely, evidences convincingly, and positions the product to solve.

Editorial Intelligence produces launch narratives by connecting signals: customer research, practitioner interviews, market data, operational evidence from sales and support. The narrative is not written to support the product. It is developed from the evidence first — which is why it lands as authoritative rather than promotional.

The Hidden Hours narrative is an example of this in practice. It was not written as a product marketing asset. It emerged from practitioner interviews about operational pressure in accounting. That origin — research-first, evidence-led — is what made it credible enough to anchor a product campaign.

### 2. The customer evidence programme

Customer evidence is the most underused asset in most product marketing programmes.

The standard approach: identify a customer with a good outcome, brief an agency to interview them, publish a case study, repeat annually.

The result is a library of case studies that describe outcomes without explaining why they happened — and a pipeline that requires a new commission for every new claim.

An evidence programme built on Editorial Intelligence works differently. Customer signals are collected continuously — from interviews, from support conversations, from sales calls, from events. The Customer Signal Framework is the operating system for that collection. Each signal is coded against the narrative it supports. Evidence accumulates against a claim rather than being published as a standalone asset.

When a launch requires customer proof, the proof already exists in a structured, usable form. The case study becomes a way of surfacing evidence that is already connected to the argument — not the source of the argument itself.

### 3. Sales positioning

Sales is often the last function to receive narrative work, and the first to develop its own version of the story when the narrative doesn't arrive in usable form.

Editorial Intelligence produces sales positioning by translating the narrative into language that works in a conversation rather than an article. The same underlying argument — the same evidence, the same claim, the same customer proof — is expressed differently for someone with five minutes in a prospect meeting than for someone reading a thought leadership piece.

The positioning is not a separate piece of work. It is a derivative of the narrative, using the same source material. This is what keeps sales and marketing telling the same story — not alignment workshops, but shared origin.

### 4. The campaign brief

Most campaign briefs describe what content needs to be produced and when.

An Editorial Intelligence campaign brief describes what story needs to be told — and gives every function a version of that story appropriate to their channel and audience.

The brief does not say "write a blog post about MTD". It says "the argument is that MTD is a structural change that separates genuinely operational practices from annual-workflow firms — here is the evidence, here are the customer signals, here are the three versions of that argument appropriate for PR, editorial and sales respectively."

Every function produces from the same argument. The outputs look different. The intelligence behind them is shared.

## How this maps to the EI Cycle

The four product marketing outputs map directly to the stages of the Editorial Intelligence Cycle:

**Signals** → Customer research, practitioner interviews, market data, sales conversations, support evidence — the raw material of both the narrative and the customer evidence programme

**Editorial Intelligence** → The analytical layer: identifying recurring patterns, connecting evidence, developing the narrative argument, prioritising the claims worth making

**Narrative development** → The launch narrative and its derivatives: the strategic argument expressed in long form, then translated into the formats each channel needs

**Activation** → The channel layer: PR, editorial, thought leadership, customer marketing, sales enablement and events — each drawing from the shared narrative foundation

**Learning** → Signals that return from activation — coverage, customer conversations, sales feedback, event responses — feeding back into the narrative and strengthening future campaigns

The activation layer is covered in detail in the [Editorial Activation](/activation) model.

## Hidden Hours as a worked example

The Hidden Hours narrative demonstrates this model in practice.

**Signals**: Practitioner interviews across accounting and bookkeeping firms, identifying a consistent pattern of time absorbed by unacknowledged work — compliance overhead, client friction, expanding advisory expectations, late payments.

**Editorial Intelligence**: The pattern connected. The story was not "accountants are busy" — it was more precise: the profession is absorbing structural change in ways that are becoming unsustainable. That precision is what makes it a narrative rather than a survey finding.

**Narrative development**: The Hidden Hours narrative became the foundation for multiple product marketing outputs — a research report, a series of thought leadership articles, customer story content structured around the same operational themes, and a pricing framework (the MTD Pricing Framework) that emerged directly from the evidence the narrative generated.

**Activation**: The narrative was activated across PR (research launch), editorial (practitioner-facing articles), customer marketing (case studies structured around the Hidden Hours themes), and events (FAB, ICB MTD Implementation Day — where the same argument was tested in real conversations and refined based on practitioner response).

**Learning**: Each activation event returned new signals. The MTD connection sharpened. The AI Maturity Curve emerged as a connected framework. The evidence base grew more dense.

The narrative did not end with the launch. It became a platform — and each new piece of evidence made the next piece of work faster to produce.

## What this requires

Building product marketing on an Editorial Intelligence foundation requires three things that most organisations do not currently have:

**A continuous signal collection process** — customer signals collected and structured against a narrative, not commissioned campaign by campaign. The Customer Signal Framework describes how to build this.

**Editorial judgement at the centre** — someone who can read signals, identify patterns, develop arguments and distinguish between evidence that strengthens the narrative and evidence that is merely interesting. This is not a content production role. It is an editorial intelligence role.

**A narrative library, not a content library** — assets organised by the argument they support, not by format or channel. A library structured this way allows any function to find the evidence they need for any channel without commissioning new work.

## The commercial case

The commercial case for Editorial Intelligence in product marketing is not primarily about cost reduction, though that is a consequence.

It is about compounding returns.

Activity-based product marketing produces a flat return curve. Each campaign costs roughly the same to produce and reaches roughly the same audience. The organisation gets better at producing campaigns but not at accumulating authority.

Narrative-based product marketing produces a compounding return curve. Each piece of work builds on the evidence and authority established by the last. The organisation develops a recognisable position in its market. The cost per credible claim falls over time because the evidence base is already built.

Hidden Hours did not become more credible because more money was spent on it. It became more credible because more evidence was added to it — from customer stories, from regulatory change, from practitioner conversations, from AI adoption data. Each addition cost less than the last because the narrative infrastructure was already in place.

That is what Editorial Intelligence makes possible: a product marketing programme that gets better over time rather than resetting with each campaign.

## Starting points

**For an organisation that does not yet have a narrative foundation**

Begin with signal collection. Run six practitioner or customer interviews using the Customer Signal Framework. Look for the pattern that recurs — the problem that appears in different forms across different conversations. That pattern is the beginning of a narrative.

**For an organisation with existing research that is not being activated**

The research is probably telling a story that has not been articulated. Map the findings against the audience's known operational pressures. Identify the most precise, specific claim the evidence supports. That claim, developed and evidenced, is the launch narrative.

**For an organisation where sales and marketing are telling different stories**

The problem is almost always that the narrative was never translated into sales language. Take the existing narrative and produce three versions: the long-form thought leadership version, the two-paragraph version for a sales deck, and the one-sentence version for a cold outreach subject line. All three should be arguments, not descriptions. If they cannot be translated, the narrative is not yet specific enough.
