The buyer journey has fundamentally shifted.

Where it was once search → click → visit → conversion, it is increasingly: AI discovery → Google validation → website conversion.

Over 9 in 10 C-suite executives and senior leaders use AI search tools daily. 94% of B2B buyers now use generative AI at some point during the purchasing process. And 54% of buyers say they would hold a negative opinion of a brand that doesn't appear in AI search — before a single click is made.

AI systems are now reputation engines for lead generation. They synthesise information from across an organisation's entire digital footprint — owned content, press coverage, trade body membership, industry databases, social media, customer reviews, crowdsourced platforms — and form a view of the brand. That view shapes buyer consideration before they ever visit a website.

Most discussion about AI visibility begins with optimisation — how to be found once that process is already underway.

Editorial Intelligence begins much earlier.

It asks: how does an organisation continuously generate trustworthy knowledge that deserves to be discovered?

That knowledge becomes evidence. Evidence becomes reputation. Reputation becomes AI visibility. AI visibility contributes to Buyability.

This framework explains how those four things connect — and why the organisations that understand the sequence will have a compounding advantage over those that jump straight to optimisation.

---

## Why AI visibility is not just GEO

Generative Engine Optimisation is a legitimate discipline. It improves discoverability. It helps organisations understand where they appear inside AI systems, and how to structure content so that AI can interpret it accurately.

But GEO is primarily an optimisation discipline. It works on what already exists.

Editorial Intelligence asks an earlier question: how do organisations continuously create knowledge worth optimising in the first place?

The distinction matters because you cannot optimise your way to authority. You can only optimise what is already there. If the underlying knowledge is thin, fragmented or disconnected, optimisation makes it more discoverable — but does not make it more credible.

Brands with the strongest media presence are 80% more likely to be recommended by AI. Visitors arriving from LLMs are worth 4.4 times more than traditional organic search visitors. The signal quality of what an organisation has built — not just its technical structure — determines whether AI recommends it.

**GEO asks:** how do we improve discoverability of what we have?

**Editorial Intelligence asks:** how do we build knowledge systems that deserve to be discovered?

The two are not in competition. But the sequence matters. Editorial Intelligence sits upstream.

| GEO | Editorial Intelligence |
|-----|----------------------|
| Audit visibility | Capture organisational knowledge |
| Optimise entities | Connect evidence across sources |
| Improve structure | Build reusable narratives |
| Improve technical signals | Create authority through consistency |
| — | Compound learning over time |

## The Evidence Engine

The Evidence Engine is one of the core Editorial Intelligence frameworks. It describes how organisations turn raw organisational knowledge into reusable evidence — the material that fuels every downstream output.

Most organisations already have more knowledge than they realise. What they lack is the editorial infrastructure to capture, connect and maintain it.

**Evidence sources include:**

Customer research · Customer interviews · Customer stories · Events and advisory boards · Sales conversations · SME expertise · Support conversations · Community insight · Industry change · Policy · Product usage data · Market intelligence

Editorial Intelligence sits between raw knowledge and published content. It:

- captures signals from across the organisation
- connects evidence that would otherwise remain isolated
- synthesises findings into coherent positions
- prioritises what deserves activation
- maintains and updates knowledge as it evolves

> Evidence only compounds when it is connected.

An organisation that captures good research but never connects it to its customer stories, sales conversations and event insight is not building an Evidence Engine. It is building a series of disconnected assets that reset with every campaign.

## Narrative Architecture

Isolated content creates isolated signals. Connected narratives create organisational memory.

The most important editorial shift an organisation can make is from thinking about content as individual outputs to thinking about it as a connected architecture. Every piece of content should reinforce every other piece.

Research → thought leadership → customer stories → newsletter → events → video → PR → sales enablement → website → frameworks

When these are genuinely connected — when the customer story draws on the same research that informed the thought leadership, which was tested at the event, which was refined through sales conversations — the organisation builds something that individual pieces cannot: a coherent and consistent position that accumulates over time.

That accumulated position is what AI systems can infer from. Not a single optimised page. A body of connected evidence that points in the same direction.

## From evidence to reputation

Evidence only creates value when it is activated — distributed through the channels where buyers and AI systems encounter it.

**Activation channels include:**

Articles and website · PR and media · Thought leadership · LinkedIn · Newsletters · Podcasts · Customer stories · Video · Events · Diagnostics · Frameworks · Communities · Industry bodies

The goal of activation is not reach alone. It is repeated validation across the places buyers and AI systems already trust.

A customer story published once on a website creates one signal. The same customer story referenced in a newsletter, cited in a PR piece, discussed at an event, linked from a framework and shared by the customer themselves creates a pattern of validation that is qualitatively different.

That pattern is reputation. And reputation, built consistently over time, is what AI systems are increasingly inferring from.

## The four pillars of AI visibility

AI visibility is not a single thing. It is the outcome of four capabilities working together.

**Visibility**
People and AI can find your expertise. Your knowledge is structured, accessible and consistently present where buyers look.

**Consistency**
The same narrative appears across every touchpoint. Research, events, customer stories, thought leadership and sales conversations all point in the same direction.

**Relationships**
Customers, journalists, analysts, communities, employees and industry bodies reinforce your reputation. Third-party validation creates signals that self-published content cannot.

**Credibility**
Claims are supported by evidence, methodology and demonstrated experience. Assertions without evidence create weak signals. Evidence-backed positions create strong ones.

Editorial Intelligence explains how organisations build all four pillars systematically — not through isolated campaigns, but through a connected knowledge system that accumulates over time.

## AI visibility

AI systems do not simply retrieve pages and rank them. The mechanism is fundamentally different from classic search.

Classic search: crawl, index, serve. Visibility came from keywords, technical health and links.

AI search: training data, query fan-out, chunk-level retrieval, synthesis. Visibility comes from entities, authority signals and technical health — but the authority signals now include your entire digital footprint, not just your website.

AI systems synthesise information from press coverage, owned content, trade body membership, industry databases, social media, crowdsourced platforms and customer reviews. Every signal counts. Every part of your digital presence tells a story — and AI is reading all of it simultaneously.

> AI is an inference machine. Inconsistent signals don't just create confusion; they create risk.

Fragmented organisations create fragmented signals. A good research report disconnected from customer evidence, disconnected from thought leadership, disconnected from third-party validation, creates a weak overall inference — regardless of how well any individual piece is optimised. Worse, inconsistent signals can lead to inaccuracies or hallucinations that misrepresent the brand.

Connected organisations create stronger inferences. When the same position appears consistently across owned content, earned media, industry bodies and customer advocacy — when the narrative is coherent across the full digital footprint — AI systems have reliable signal to work with.

This is not a claim about how any specific AI system ranks content — the mechanisms are neither fully transparent nor stable. It is an observation about the general principle: coherent, connected, evidence-based knowledge generates stronger authority signals than scattered, inconsistent or claim-heavy content.

## Buyability

Research from LinkedIn and Bain & Company defines Buyability as the collective confidence the Buyer Group must feel before it commits to a purchase. It is an emotional threshold, not a rational one.

Complex B2B buying is rarely decided by product capability alone. The research found that 3 out of the top 5 decision drivers are about group dynamics, not product features. The single highest-ranked emotional driver was: *I felt I could defend the decision even if it went wrong.*

Buyers want a defensible decision. That is what Buyability provides.

Editorial Intelligence connects to Buyability through evidence. Evidence creates the confidence that buyers need — confidence that the organisation understands their problem, has helped others like them, and can demonstrate that capability rather than just claim it.

The same research found that vendors are 20 times more likely to be bought when known across the whole Buyer Group than when championed only by product experts. Editorial Intelligence helps organisations communicate evidence in ways that reach the whole buying committee — not just the technical expert, but finance, legal, procurement and operations.

---

## The hidden buyer

Most B2B content is written for the person with the problem. It addresses the practitioner, the category expert, the person who identified the need.

But the LinkedIn/Bain research found that Hidden Buyers — the Finance, Legal, Procurement and Operations functions who sit in the Buyer Group without product expertise — account for approximately 50% of total decision-making influence.

A vendor known only to the recommending function is dramatically less likely to be purchased than one known across the whole group.

Editorial Intelligence helps organisations build the kind of broad, evidence-based reputation that reaches beyond product experts. Not by abandoning category expertise, but by connecting that expertise to the evidence that builds confidence across a wider audience.

When your customer stories demonstrate real outcomes for organisations like theirs, when your frameworks show a clear methodology, when your research demonstrates genuine understanding of their context — those signals reach the hidden buyer too.

## Customer advocacy as evidence

Customer stories are not marketing assets.

They are reputation assets — and the most powerful form of evidence an organisation can activate.

The LinkedIn/Bain research found that buyers are three times more likely to buy a product heavily recommended by customers than one that promises to be better or cheaper. Peer recommendation outperforms product claims consistently.

Customer stories do more than demonstrate outcomes. They demonstrate:

- that the relationship was real, not theoretical
- that implementation worked, not just the promise
- that an organisation similar to theirs trusted this vendor
- that the risk of the decision was manageable

For AI systems, customer stories are also evidence of demonstrated capability — not asserted capability. A page that claims expertise creates one type of signal. A library of customer stories that consistently demonstrate expertise, linked to the research that informed the approach, creates a pattern of evidence that is qualitatively more authoritative.

The organisations that treat customer stories as strategic assets — not campaign content — build a compound advantage in both buyer confidence and AI visibility.

## The Editorial Intelligence operating model

The full model shows how each layer connects to the next — and how the system compounds over time.

```
Organisational Knowledge
        ↓
Evidence Engine
        ↓
Editorial Intelligence
        ↓
Narrative Architecture
        ↓
Content Activation
        ↓
Digital Reputation
        ↓
AI Visibility
        ↓
Buyer Confidence
        ↓
Buyability
        ↓
Commercial Outcomes
        ↓
Marketing Intelligence
        ↓
[feeds back into Organisational Knowledge]
```

The feedback loop is what makes the system compound rather than reset. Commercial outcomes generate new customer evidence. Customer evidence feeds back into the Evidence Engine. The Evidence Engine generates better narratives. Better narratives create stronger reputation. Stronger reputation improves AI visibility. Improved AI visibility builds Buyability.

Each cycle makes the next cycle more effective.

## What changes for B2B marketing

The implication of this framework is a shift in how marketing teams allocate attention and investment.

**From** publishing more content → **to** building stronger evidence systems

**From** campaign thinking → **to** knowledge compounding

**From** SEO as the primary discovery discipline → **to** Editorial Intelligence as the upstream capability that makes GEO, AI visibility and Buyability possible

**From** content for the product expert → **to** evidence that reaches the whole buying committee

**From** customer stories as campaign assets → **to** customer advocacy as strategic reputation infrastructure

None of this means abandoning what works. Optimisation, campaign activation, demand generation — these remain important. But they work best when they are built on a foundation of connected, evidence-based knowledge that has been maintained, updated and consistently expressed over time.

## Key principles

**Evidence creates reputation.**
Consistent, connected, evidence-based knowledge is what builds the authority that buyers and AI systems infer from. Claims create weak signals. Evidence creates strong ones.

**Reputation creates recommendation.**
The LinkedIn/Bain research is unambiguous: recommendations drive Buyability more than product capability or price. Reputation is the foundation of recommendation. Editorial Intelligence builds that foundation systematically.

**Recommendation creates Buyability.**
When buyers can defend a decision, when the Buyer Group is aligned, when peer validation is available — the emotional threshold for commitment is met. Evidence is what makes that possible across the whole buying committee.

**Editorial Intelligence creates the evidence that makes all three possible.**
It is not a content strategy. It is not an SEO discipline. It is the organisational operating system that determines whether knowledge compounds into authority — or resets with every campaign.
