The Editorial Intelligence Framework describes the process by which organisations transform what they already know into strategic assets that compound in value over time.

It is built around a cycle — not a linear workflow, but a continuous loop in which each pass produces evidence that strengthens the next.

## The cycle

**Signals → Synthesis → Narratives → Activation → Evidence → Learning and reuse → Signals**

Each stage has a distinct character and requires a distinct kind of work.

### Signals

Signals are observations that have the potential to change or strengthen an organisation's strategic understanding.

They are everywhere: in customer interviews, research findings, product feedback, sales conversations, webinar and event discussions, video comments, social responses, market observations. Most organisations generate signals continuously. The challenge is not producing them — it is recognising which ones matter.

That recognition is the first exercise of editorial judgement.

### Synthesis

Signals become insights through synthesis — the recognition that multiple observations from different sources are pointing at the same underlying truth.

A single customer describing workflow friction is a signal. Fifty customers describing the same friction, in the same terms, across different firm sizes and geographies — that is an insight. It reveals something true about how a category of problem is experienced.

Not every signal survives synthesis. The editorial judgement required here is the capacity to sit with incomplete evidence long enough to recognise what it is actually revealing, without forcing a pattern that isn't there.

### Narratives

Narratives are where organisational knowledge becomes strategic direction.

An insight explains something. A narrative argues for something — a position the organisation can build on, develop over time, and defend with evidence. Narratives provide the organising structure that connects evidence across time, teams and channels.

Narratives are the centre of the framework. Everything before them exists to discover them. Everything after them exists to strengthen them.

### Activation

Narratives are activated through formats and channels suited to the audience and purpose: articles, frameworks, diagnostics, case studies, AI workflows, playbooks, video, social content, email newsletters, event presentations, sales enablement, executive communications.

Format and channel selection is a strategic decision, not an afterthought. The question is not "how do we produce content?" but "which form will make this narrative most useful to this audience, in this context?"

The same insight should be expressible as a long-form article, a social post, a webinar session, a sales conversation and a customer story. The investment in the underlying synthesis is made once. The activation is adapted for context. This is not content atomisation — it is purposeful format selection guided by editorial judgement.

### Evidence

Every activation generates new evidence.

A published article surfaces questions the original research didn't ask. A social post reveals what resonates and what prompts disagreement. A webinar generates new practitioner insights from the room. A diagnostic reveals patterns in how the problem is experienced. A framework, tested in practice, either holds or requires refinement.

That evidence feeds back into the cycle as new signals. The narrative is refined. The position is strengthened. The next pass is more valuable than the previous one.

### Learning and reuse

The final stage closes the loop.

Evidence is applied: the narrative is refined where it needs sharpening, high-performing elements are repurposed in new contexts, and the signal capture capability that feeds into the next cycle improves. What worked is understood. What didn't is examined.

The result is an organisation that does not simply produce better content over time — it becomes more intelligent, building an editorial capability that compounds rather than resets with each new campaign.

## Applying the framework

The framework can be entered at any point in the cycle.

Some organisations begin with a question — they know what position they want to own and need to find the evidence that supports it. Others begin with evidence — they have research or customer stories and need to understand what narrative they support. Others begin with a narrative — they have a strategic story but lack the systematic approach to develop it over time.

The framework is not a prescription. It is a structure for recognising where you are in the cycle and what the next step should be.

## The asymmetry of the cycle

One property of the cycle is worth understanding: signals are abundant, insights are scarce, and narratives are rarer still.

Thousands of signals are generated continuously. From those, a handful of genuine insights emerge — patterns that reveal something strategically significant. From those insights, one or two narratives are worth building — positions that the organisation can own, defend, and develop over years.

This asymmetry explains why editorial judgement is the bottleneck in the system. The scarcest resource is not information. It is the capacity to recognise which information matters and what it means.
