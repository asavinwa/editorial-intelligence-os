The work isn't new. Someone has always had to sit across research, customer conversations, events and market signal, and turn scattered evidence into a position an organisation can actually act on. What's changed isn't the need for that. It's the cost.

Hidden Hours didn't turn into ten months of evolving content because AI generated an insight that wasn't already sitting in the data — the finding was there the moment the results came back. What changed was the cost of revisiting that dataset repeatedly: reshaping it for a new angle, holding some of it back deliberately for a better moment, testing a framing against the evidence before committing to it. A research team re-cutting the same survey six different ways used to be an indefensible use of anyone's time. Now it's just Tuesday.

Accountex made the same point in real time. Ten pieces came out of one event, not because ten insights were sitting there waiting to be found, but because the same tension kept surfacing across sessions that had nothing to do with each other on paper. Spotting that is judgement. Turning it into ten properly differentiated pieces while the event still mattered is only possible because drafting got cheap enough to do at that speed. A few years ago, that pattern recognition would have produced one piece, published after the moment had already passed.

Which is where I think this actually goes. Production is commoditising fast, and no amount of "craft still matters" is going to hold that line. What doesn't commoditise is knowing what the evidence supports, what's distinctive enough to be worth saying, and what should be connected that currently isn't. That's not a faster version of the old content job. It's a different job that's been sitting inside the old one, underpriced, because nobody could see it clearly enough to charge for it separately.

Accounting firms are already living this — MTD expanded the scope of the work well before anyone updated how it's priced. Marketing organisations are about to do it to themselves. They'll get the same production tools everyone else gets, and quietly not build a title or a budget line for whoever's actually doing the synthesis, because on paper it still looks like the same job.

It won't, for much longer.
