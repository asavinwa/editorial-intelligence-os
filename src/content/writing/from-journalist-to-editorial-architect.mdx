I have had five job titles. They look like a career progression. They are actually a single argument, developed over twenty years.

---

## 1. Journalist (2003–2007)

Journalism teaches you things that most content roles do not.

It teaches you to find evidence rather than assume it. To ask questions that reveal something rather than confirm what you already believe. To hold information to account — to look for what is missing, what contradicts, what the source has a reason to conceal.

It teaches you that a story is not what happened. A story is the argument you make about what happened, grounded in evidence that a reader can evaluate. The facts are not the story. The judgement about which facts matter, in what order, with what emphasis — that is the story.

I did not know at the time that this was the foundation of everything that came after. It felt like a job. It was actually a way of thinking.

---

## 2. Content writer (2007–2014)

The move from journalism to content writing felt like a step sideways. In some ways it was.

But it revealed something journalism had not: that most organisations had more to say than they knew how to say it. The research existed. The customer insight existed. The market evidence existed. What was missing was the editorial capacity to turn it into something coherent.

I spent seven years learning how to write for organisations — how to find the argument inside the brief, how to make complex ideas accessible, how to match register and tone to audience and context.

What I did not yet have was a system for connecting one piece of work to the next.

---

## 3. Content strategist (2014–2018)

Strategy was where the pattern became visible.

Working at scale — across multiple campaigns, multiple research programmes, multiple teams — I could see what was being lost. Each project produced evidence that was used once and then archived. Each campaign was built on research that had been commissioned for that campaign and would not inform the next one. Each event produced insight that was captured in a recap nobody read.

The work was good. The accumulation was not happening.

I started asking a question I could not yet answer: what would it look like if the evidence compounded instead of reset?

---

## 4. Editorial Intelligence designer (2018–2023)

The answer emerged gradually, and mostly from practice rather than theory.

At Sage, working on programmes like [Hidden Hours](/narratives/hidden-hours), [Winning in Small](/narratives/winning-in-small) and the [AI narrative sequence](/narratives/generative-ai), I found what the system actually looked like: research shaped into strategic narratives, activated across formats, tested through evidence, and fed back into future work. Not a content production system. A knowledge system.

Editorial Intelligence became the name for that practice: the discipline of turning organisational knowledge into intelligence that compounds over time.

The frameworks on this site — the Loop, the OS model, the evidence architecture — are the documentation of that practice. Not invented as theory. Derived from doing the work.

---

## 5. AI orchestration designer (2024–beyond)

Agentic AI changed the stakes.

When AI systems can complete meaningful work autonomously — briefing, drafting, synthesising, recommending — the quality of the knowledge they are working from becomes the determining factor in the quality of what they produce. An agent without structured context improvises. An agent with a well-built editorial operating system compounds.

The work is now about building that operating system: the frameworks, evidence libraries, narrative logic and defined positions that allow AI-assisted workflows to produce outputs that reflect the organisation's actual intelligence rather than generic capability.

The title has changed. The thread has not.

Curiosity about what is actually true. Evidence before narrative. Clarity about what the argument is. Impact from knowledge that is used, not just produced. Systems that make the next piece of work smarter than the last.

Different titles. Same thread.
