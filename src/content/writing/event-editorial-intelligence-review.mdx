*Signal note — internal review document: Winning in Small: Event, Research and Editorial Intelligence Review. June 2026. Confidential.*

---

This review documents how events, research and customer insight were turned into editorial output across the Winning in Small programme. It covers 32 published content assets, two major editorial narratives, and a strategic framework — and it includes performance data that shows what connected editorial systems actually produce compared to standalone articles.

The methodology case is stronger when the evidence is visible. That's what this review provides.

---

## Where the insight came from

Four input types fed the Winning in Small editorial strategy:

**Hidden Hours research** — 1,000 UK accountants and bookkeepers. The anchor dataset: core compliance down from 50% to 44% of the working week, beyond-the-brief work up from 9% to 13%, 81% regularly taking on out-of-scope work, 70% saying fees don't reflect full support. The strongest proof layer.

**Winning in Small survey** — 960 UK small businesses and 200 accountants. Quantitative insight on AI adoption, MTD expectations and software decisions. Most accountants expect AI to benefit practice; accountants are highly influential in software decisions.

**Customer insight** — sole trader, UX and public affairs research. Sole traders show lower confidence around tax and reporting. They seek clarity, guidance, reassurance and control. MTD increases the need for confidence-building. Value means reducing uncertainty, not adding features.

**Practitioner insight** — Accountex, Sage Future, practitioner conversations, community feedback. Operational pressure recurring across all conversations. AI adoption linked to trust and accountability. Visibility and control as recurring themes.

The synthesis flow: research + customer insight + events + practitioner feedback → [Winning in Small](/narratives/winning-in-small) editorial strategy → articles, frameworks, tools, social, video, customer stories, advocacy, campaigns.

## The editorial intelligence model

The five-stage model that turned signals into narratives:

```
01 Capture
Signal from events, research, conversations

02 Frame
Human interpretation — what matters, what's shifting

03 Pattern
Recurring themes identified across sources

04 Build
Framework constructed from convergent signals

05 Activate
Connected content system deployed
```

The key word is *convergent*. Winning in Small wasn't a creative positioning exercise. It emerged from recurring signals appearing consistently across research, events, customer interviews and practitioner conversations — operational pressure, visibility gap, continuous compliance, trusted AI, hidden work.

When the same theme appears in survey data and a Accountex session and a sole trader interview and a practitioner conversation, it has earned its place in the narrative.

## Two content systems, not 32 standalone articles

The review documents 32 published assets — but the more significant finding is structural: those assets organised into two connected editorial systems, not a collection of individual pieces.

**[Winning in Small](/narratives/winning-in-small)** — pressure, visibility and control. 16 assets: Hidden Hours research, MTD Responsibility series, HMRC Future, Late Payments, diagnostic quiz, EFL strategy, editorial roadmap.

**[Trusted AI](/narratives/trusted-ai)** — capability, trust and accountability. 15 assets: Sage Future, Agentic AI programme, Authentic Intelligence, AI Small Business Guide, AI Workbook, AI in Practice, AI Trust.

The distinction matters because it changes how you measure value. A standalone article has reach and a lifespan. A content system has reach, reuse, compound value, sales enablement application and AI search visibility — and each new piece in the system makes the existing pieces stronger.

## Performance signals

The review tracked performance across the two content systems. A few patterns emerged that hold beyond the specific numbers.

**MTD and operational pressure content** generated the strongest reach and conversion — particularly where newsletter distribution supported the piece. Content with a clear, specific audience problem (compliance readiness, pricing pressure, client behaviour) consistently outperformed broader editorial framing.

**AI narrative content** had lower reach but generated stronger form engagement relative to sessions — evidence that emerging thought leadership topics attract a more active audience even at lower volume. People who found the AI content were more likely to act on it.

**Downloadable assets** performed well without dedicated promotion when the surrounding content gave them context. The MTD playbook circulated across related articles through internal links alone.

The pattern across all three: editorial content built on a real narrative foundation and supported by distribution outperforms content that has either element alone — strong narrative without distribution, or promotion without the underlying argument.

## The finding about distribution

The most significant strategic finding in the review isn't about content. It's about activation.

> "We are not undersupplied on content — we are undersupplied on activation."

Several high-value assets generated strong results with minimal promotional support. The constraint isn't content quality or volume. It's the systematic routing of those assets through the channels that would extend their reach: newsletter, Sage Download, social, video, campaigns.

This is an editorial intelligence finding, not just a distribution recommendation. If the measurement model only counts sessions on publication week, it will consistently undervalue evergreen content, misread the strategy as underperforming, and produce pressure for more content rather than better activation.

The right response is a lightweight model for identifying high-value event-derived assets and routing them to amplification channels — not more content.

## The emerging opportunity: Embedded Services

The review names Embedded Services as the next major narrative gap in the Winning in Small strategy:

> "Embedded Services represents the next major narrative gap in Sage's market position. Identified alongside AI and regulation as a major shift shaping small business finance, Embedded Services has no editorial programme yet — making it the most significant untapped narrative in the Winning in Small strategy. Trusted AI and Operational Pressure each took 12–18 months to become mature narratives. Embedded Services needs the same investment now — before competitors frame it first."

The themes: simplicity, connected finance, reduced fragmentation, visibility, operational control.

The editorial intelligence observation here is about timing. Narratives are not built quickly. The ones that look established now — Operational Pressure, Trusted AI — were started early enough to develop properly. Embedded Services is at the point in its development where the investment needs to begin.

## Strategic findings

Seven findings from the review that hold beyond the Winning in Small programme:

1. Event-derived content creates thought leadership value by interpreting market change — not just reporting it.
2. Events and research generate content programmes, not just standalone articles.
3. Narrative frameworks increase the value extracted from events, research and customer insight.
4. Promotion and reuse act as multipliers for strategic content.
5. Original insight is increasingly valuable in an AI search environment.
6. High-value evergreen assets should be optimised for GEO/AEO, internal linking and reuse.
7. Similar themes appearing across multiple teams suggests broader organisational relevance.
