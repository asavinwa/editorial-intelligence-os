Every piece of journalism contains two stories.

The story that happened.

And the story we decide to tell.

Those aren't always the same thing.

Watching the reaction to England's win over DR Congo reminded me how often narrative wins.

Within minutes, the headlines had settled.

England were "rescued." Harry Kane "saved" Thomas Tuchel. England were "15 minutes from one of the greatest humiliations in their history."

There's some truth in all of that. England were below their best for long periods. Kane produced two decisive moments. Tuchel still has tactical questions to answer.

But something else happened.

Almost nobody talked about DR Congo.

Not really.

DR Congo became supporting actors in England's story. That isn't unique to this match. It's something football journalism does remarkably often.

---

## The what-if problem

Most of the coverage was built around events that never happened.

England were *nearly* knocked out. Tuchel was *close* to disaster. England *almost* suffered one of their greatest humiliations.

Compelling hooks. Also hypothetical.

Football analysis increasingly starts from an alternative timeline rather than the match that was actually played. What if Kane hadn't scored? What if DR Congo had held on? Those are interesting conversations. They aren't analysis.

Analysis begins with the evidence.

England won. Why? How? What changed?

Instead, we construct a counterfactual and use it as the organising principle for everything that follows.

---

## The disappearing opponent

The more interesting omission wasn't England. It was DR Congo.

Before the game, the evidence was already there. They had drawn with Portugal. They had only lost to Colombia through a late goal. They had qualified from a competitive group. Their squad contains players competing across Europe's major leagues.

None of that makes them favourites against England. But it should change the frame.

Instead, almost all of that context disappeared after the final whistle.

The match became evidence of England's shortcomings rather than evidence that England had faced a capable side.

That distinction matters. Because it changes the questions journalists ask.

---

## Different opponents, different analysis

Imagine Manchester City taken to extra time by Sunderland in the FA Cup. Or Liverpool struggling to beat Coventry.

The post-match analysis usually begins with the smaller side. How did they press? How did they defend? What tactical problems did they create?

The underdog gets tactical agency. They're recognised as active participants in the match.

Now compare that with England against an African nation.

The discussion shifts rapidly inward. England lacked creativity. England looked vulnerable. Tuchel doesn't know his best team. England escaped.

Those observations may contain truth. But notice what disappears.

DR Congo become a measuring stick rather than an opponent. Very little attention is paid to the questions on the other side. How did they disrupt England's build-up? Why were they effective in transition? Where did they create overloads? How did they limit England between the lines?

The analysis becomes strangely one-sided.

---

## The hierarchy that persists

Football has changed. Players move through the same academies. Managers study the same coaching courses. Tactical ideas spread globally within weeks.

Many African national teams now contain Champions League players, Premier League players, footballers developed inside Europe's elite systems. The quality gap has narrowed considerably.

The editorial assumptions haven't always kept pace.

When England struggle against Germany, Spain or France, the explanation is usually that England encountered an excellent team. When England struggle against an African nation, the default explanation often becomes that England underperformed.

Not because journalists consciously disrespect African football. But because inherited assumptions still shape the stories we instinctively reach for.

---

## Selective context

Germany needed penalties to beat Paraguay. The conversation focused on Paraguay's resilience.

The Netherlands needed penalties to eliminate Morocco. Morocco were credited with an excellent game plan.

Those performances became evidence that tournament football is difficult.

England needing two late goals against DR Congo became evidence that England were failing.

Same tournament. Different editorial frame.

The difference isn't necessarily bias. It's narrative selection. Editors choose which facts become the story.

---

## Narrative versus analysis

Narrative asks: what is the simplest story?

Analysis asks: what explains what happened?

Those aren't the same question.

Narrative reduces complexity. Analysis embraces it. Narrative wants heroes and villains. Analysis wants mechanisms.

Narrative says Harry Kane rescued England. Analysis asks why England created sustained pressure over the final twenty minutes.

Narrative says England nearly collapsed. Analysis asks why DR Congo's transitions became less effective after the substitutions.

One produces emotion. The other produces understanding. The best journalism manages both.

---

## This isn't just about football

One reason I enjoy football is that it exposes editorial habits that exist everywhere else.

Business journalism often works the same way. Political journalism certainly does. Technology journalism is full of narratives in search of evidence to support them.

Editorial Intelligence starts somewhere different.

Start with the evidence. Look for patterns. Understand the mechanisms. Only then decide what story the evidence actually supports.

Not the story you hoped to tell before the game began.

---

## Better questions

England may lose to Mexico. They may reach the semi-finals. Neither outcome changes the point.

Football deserves better questions.

Not *"how close were England to disaster?"* — but *"what actually happened?"*

Not *"who rescued England?"* — but *"why did the game evolve the way it did?"*

And perhaps most importantly: *"what evidence are we ignoring because it doesn't fit the narrative?"*

That's where Editorial Intelligence begins.

Not with the headline. With the evidence.

The story comes afterwards.
